# BPA in Thermal Receipts and Human Health

> How bisphenol&nbsp;A from till and credit-card receipts gets into the body, what it does to human health, and how real and how large the risk is — for the average person versus cashiers. This is the whole picture, **ranked by how strongly the evidence holds** — settled exposure facts first, the genuinely-contested disease-causation question last.

**Complete** · Published 25 June 2026 · *Rebuilt 14 July 2026 — every cited study re-read from source and re-verified* · **29 cited studies & official assessments** · Confidence: **High** on exposure · **Moderate** on harm magnitude.

!!! abstract "TL;DR"
    Thermal receipts are coated in **free, rub-off BPA** (≈19.6&nbsp;mg/g, in 44% of receipts) that absorbs **through the skin** — a route that bypasses liver detox, so more reaches the blood in its **active form** and lingers for days. **Hand sanitizer amplifies absorption up to 185×.** Diet is still the average person's main BPA source, so a stray receipt is a **small, easily-avoided** add-on — but for **cashiers** it's a real occupational exposure (~2–3× higher urinary BPA, with DNA-oxidative-damage and insulin-resistance correlates). "BPA-free" receipts are usually BPS/BPF — no safer. EFSA (2023) cut the safe dose ~20,000× and says background diet already exceeds it; the US FDA still calls it safe and doesn't regulate receipts at all. **Verdict: avoidance is cheap and rational — strongly so for cashiers and pregnant women.**

!!! tip "The picture, ranked by how strongly the evidence holds"
    - **Tier 1 — settled (multi-study + agency):** receipts carry **mg-level free rub-off BPA**;[^S1] **gloves abolish** the exposure (proof it's the skin);[^S2][^S6] the dermal route is **more bioactive + slower-clearing** than diet;[^S4] **cashiers run ~2–3× elevated** across three cohorts;[^S3][^S5][^S6] **"BPA-free" = BPS/BPF**, as hormonally active as BPA;[^S13] **EFSA cut the safe dose ~20,000×** and diet already exceeds it.[^S15]
    - **Tier 2 — well-supported:** hand-sanitizer amplifies absorption (~up to 185×);[^S1] one worst-case event ≈ the population 95th percentile;[^S1] BPA lowers sperm + bioactive androgen (18-study meta);[^S7] **diet/canned food dominates for the average person** (so a stray receipt is a small add-on);[^S26][^S29] the EU restricts receipts, the US does not.[^S19][^S17]
    - **Tier 3 — consistent but observational:** low-dose organ effects (CLARITY academic);[^S12] prenatal hyperactivity (presumed hazard);[^S11] a modest CVD signal;[^S10] BPS/BPF obesity/diabetes epidemiology;[^S24] placental cytotoxicity in vitro.[^S28]
    - **Tier 4 — genuinely contested:** whether the receipt fraction *causes* a specific disease turns on the unresolved **EFSA-vs-FDA/BfR** dispute.[^S16] Verdict: **real exposure, contested harm, cheap avoidance — precaution is rational, strongly for cashiers and pregnant women.**

## Tier 1 — Settled: the exposure is real

**Bisphenol A (BPA)** is a high-volume industrial chemical and a confirmed **endocrine disruptor** — it binds estrogen receptors and acts as a weak exogenous estrogen, with additional anti-androgenic and metabolic activity. In thermal receipt paper it is not a bound polymer (as in polycarbonate plastic or epoxy can-linings) but a **free, powdery colour developer** coating the surface — so it rubs off onto skin on contact.

**Receipts carry milligram quantities of free BPA.** In a US survey of 50 thermal receipts, **22 (44%) carried high BPA at 19.6 ± 1.0&nbsp;mg per gram of paper** (≈1–2% of the paper's weight) — thousands of times more free BPA than migrates from a plastic bottle; BPS, the main substitute, was found in 52% of receipts.[^S1]

**Gloves abolish the exposure — the cleanest proof it's the skin.** Holding receipts for **2 hours without gloves** raised volunteers' urinary BPA from **1.8 → 5.8&nbsp;µg/L** (peak 11.1 at 8&nbsp;h); **nitrile gloves abolished the rise entirely**,[^S2] and a separate cashier intervention found the ~2× post-shift rise vanished with gloves.[^S6]

**The skin route bypasses first-pass detoxification — more bioactive, slower-clearing.** Dermal BPA shows **prolonged kinetics**: after a single contact, urinary excretion **rose linearly for ~2 days**, was still detectable in half of participants **after 1 week** (up to 9 days in one), with a higher unconjugated (bioactive) fraction than diet — whereas swallowed BPA clears within 24&nbsp;h.[^S4]

**Cashiers absorb most of their BPA dermally, and run ~2–3× elevated.** In cashiers, **dermal contact contributed 51.9–84% (geometric mean 70.9%)** of total urinary BPA;[^S3] French cashiers had **median total urinary BPA 8.92&nbsp;µg/L vs 3.54&nbsp;µg/L in controls**,[^S5] and Korean female cashiers showed **~2× higher post-shift urinary BPA**.[^S6] A documented occupational exposure across three independent cohorts.

**"BPA-free" usually means BPS/BPF — not safer.** A systematic review (1,370 studies screened) found **BPS and BPF are "as hormonally active as BPA,"** with estrogenic, anti-estrogenic, androgenic and anti-androgenic activity (mean estrogenic potency relative to BPA: BPF 1.07, BPS 0.32).[^S13] Worse for the dermal route, **more free (unmetabolized) BPS than BPA survives in urine after handling receipts (6.9% vs 2.7%)** because BPS resists metabolism.[^S14] Only genuinely **bisphenol-free** paper (e.g. Pergafast&nbsp;201) is a real fix — proven feasible when Switzerland mandated it (BPA-developed receipts fell **82.2% → 10.8%**).[^S21]

**EFSA cut the safe daily dose ~20,000× — and diet already exceeds it.** In 2023 EFSA set a tolerable daily intake of **0.2&nbsp;ng/kg body weight/day — ~20,000× below its 2015 temporary 4&nbsp;µg/kg** — on immune-system (Th17) evidence, concluding **mean and high dietary exposure in all age groups exceed the new TDI by two-to-three orders of magnitude**.[^S15] Receipts add a dermal, bioactive increment on top of an already-exceeded background.

## Tier 2 — Well-supported (amplifiers, magnitude, hormones, diet, regulation)

**Hand sanitizer and greasy hands are powerful amplifiers.** Penetration enhancers in sanitizers/lotions open the skin barrier: after sanitizer use, **up to 185× more BPA transferred from a receipt to a wet hand than a dry hand**.[^S1] In the worst-case protocol (sanitizer → hold receipt → eat finger-food), unconjugated **serum BPA peaked at ~7&nbsp;ng/mL** and **urinary total BPA hit ~20&nbsp;µg/g creatinine within 90&nbsp;min** — pushing a person to roughly the **NHANES 95th percentile (~11&nbsp;µg/g)** of the whole US population from a single event.[^S1]

**Putting the 20,000× in receipts.** Worst-case occupational handling (~10&nbsp;h/day) models to a dermal BPA dose of **~71&nbsp;µg/day (≈0.97&nbsp;µg/kg bw/day)**.[^S23] Hold that exposure fixed and only move the regulatory line: it is **~0.24× the 2015 temporary TDI** — Biedermann's 2010 paper literally called it **"42× below"** the limit of its day — but **~4,850× the 2023 TDI**.[^S15] *The receipt didn't change; EFSA's ~20,000× cut did.* (Illustrative order-of-magnitude; the absorbed fraction is smaller.)

**BPA lowers sperm count and bioactive androgen.** A 2024 systematic review/meta-analysis of **18 studies** found urinary BPA inversely associated with **sperm concentration (β −0.03)** and **total count (β −0.05)**, alongside **higher SHBG, higher estradiol, and lower bioactive androgen** — a coherent estrogenic/anti-androgenic signature (the below-reference sperm-quality link was "not robust").[^S7]

**Diet dominates for the average person — and it's well-quantified.** BPA is essentially absent from unprocessed food; it **migrates out of epoxy can-linings and plastic**, rising sharply with heat, sunlight and storage.[^S29] Canned **tuna** reached **up to ~105&nbsp;µg/kg** (highest after retort at 121&nbsp;°C; the lining shed up to 646&nbsp;µg/kg into a fatty simulant);[^S26] canned **sardines** carried **5.2–48.7&nbsp;µg/kg**, migration **+437%** with temperature.[^S27] So for the general public a stray receipt is a smaller add-on to the dietary baseline — it's cashiers where the dermal route dominates.

**The EU restricts receipt BPA; the US does not.** Commission Regulation (EU) 2016/2235 banned BPA in thermal paper above **0.02% from 2 January 2020**,[^S19] ECHA lists BPA as an endocrine-disruptor **SVHC**,[^S18] and the EU banned BPA in all food-contact materials in 2024;[^S17] the **US FDA does not regulate thermal receipts at all**, so there is no US receipt limit and US exposure is ~1,000× EFSA's new safe level.[^S17]

## Tier 3 — Consistent but observational (disease associations)

!!! warning "Important caveat"
    Almost all human *disease* epidemiology measures **total urinary BPA** (mostly dietary), **not** receipt-specific BPA. These studies establish what BPA at population exposure is associated with; receipts are one contributor. Causal attribution of a specific disease to receipt-borne BPA specifically is **not** established, and most associations are cross-sectional.

**Low-dose organ effects (CLARITY-BPA academic arm).** The academic laboratories reported **consistent low-dose effects at 2.5 / 25 / 250&nbsp;µg/kg/day across brain, heart, prostate, ovary, mammary and immune tissue**, "at doses relevant to human exposures," with non-monotonic dose–response — why "no effect at high dose" does not guarantee safety at realistic low doses.[^S12]

**Prenatal / childhood — the precautionary core.** A systematic review (29 rodent + 3 human studies) concluded **"early life BPA exposure is a presumed human hazard for the development of hyperactivity,"** urging precautionary reduction in pregnant women, infants and children.[^S11]

**Cardiovascular — a modest, consistent-direction signal.** The prospective **EPIC-Norfolk** study found incident **coronary artery disease OR 1.13 (95% CI 1.02–1.24) per SD urinary BPA**, attenuating to borderline after full adjustment; earlier NHANES cross-sectional data linked BPA to CVD (OR 1.39) and diabetes.[^S10] The type-2-diabetes signal is present but inconsistent — positive in a prospective Nurses' Health analysis (OR up to 2.08 in middle-aged women) and an overall-positive meta-analysis, but null in older women.[^S8][^S9]

**BPS/BPF now carry disease-outcome signals + cell toxicity.** Human biomonitoring ties urinary BPS/BPF to **obesity and type-2 diabetes** (BPS the most impactful), though the epidemiology is younger and mostly cross-sectional;[^S24][^S25] and in human **placental (JEG-Tox) cells, BPA and BPF triggered more inflammasome/apoptosis caspase activity than BPS, with BPF uniquely activating caspase-8** — direct cytotoxicity in a pregnancy-relevant tissue.[^S28] Cashier BPA also correlated with the DNA-oxidative-damage marker 8-OHdG and with insulin resistance.[^S3][^S6]

## Tier 4 — Genuinely contested (disease causation)

**Whether the receipt fraction causes a specific disease is unproven and expert-contested.** EFSA's 20,000× cut rests on an immune (Th17) endpoint that **Germany's BfR and a group of regulatory toxicologists argue is not a demonstrated adverse effect** — BfR derived a TDI **~1,000× higher**;[^S16] the FDA still reads the CLARITY **Core** study as "no clear adverse effects."[^S12][^S17] Two respected agencies disagree by ~1,000× on how dangerous BPA is at all. This is genuine expert disagreement, not a settled harm.

## Mitigation

Receipt-borne BPA is one of the **most easily avoided** exposures, and the interventions are evidence-backed (gloves abolished the urinary rise in two independent studies[^S2][^S6]).

**General public:** decline the paper receipt / choose **digital** receipts (the exact tool Dr.&nbsp;Shanna Swan recommends[^S22]); **don't handle receipts right after hand sanitizer or lotion** (the 185× amplifier[^S1]); don't let children hold receipts;[^S11] wash hands before eating.[^S1]

**Cashiers / retail workers (highest exposure):** **wear (nitrile) gloves** — the single most effective, proven control;[^S2][^S6] minimize skin time; avoid hand cream/sanitizer immediately before handling; employers should switch to **bisphenol-free thermal paper** (e.g. Pergafast&nbsp;201).[^S21]

**The honest bottom line:** the exposure is real and quantified; the disease consequence is plausible but unproven for receipts specifically; avoidance is cheap. Therefore precaution is rational — **strongly for cashiers and pregnant women, mildly for everyone else**. The case rests on **asymmetry of risk** (cheap to avoid, plausibly harmful), not on a demonstrated receipt-to-disease causal chain.
