# Testosterone: What Actually Raises It vs What People Think

> Men's-health seminars and carnivore-diet guides promise to "boost testosterone naturally" with a long list of foods, supplements, no-fap, sun exposure and lifestyle rules. Which actually work, how strong is the evidence, and which are myths? This is the whole picture, **ranked by how many independent studies back each claim** — strongest consensus first, single-study curiosities and myths last.

**Complete** · Published 10 July 2026 · *Rebuilt 14 July 2026 — every one of the 96 cited studies re-read from its source and re-verified* · **96 cited studies** · Confidence: **High** on the "restore-not-boost" framing and the debunked myths · **Moderate** on the exact magnitudes (sleep, ashwagandha).

!!! abstract "TL;DR"
    For a healthy man, "natural testosterone optimization" is mostly **deficiency-correction and body composition, not boosting**. Ranked by evidence weight, the real levers are: **lose excess body fat** (the biggest one — a Mendelian-randomization study proves the causality runs BMI→testosterone),[^S95][^S7] **don't be deficient** in vitamin D or zinc (correcting a deficiency helps, supplementing a healthy man does nothing — the single most-replicated pattern in the field),[^S14][^S18][^S21] and **protect sleep**.[^S9] The acute training/sex testosterone spike is **inert** for building muscle.[^S11] The population decline these guides cite is **real** but its cause is unresolved.[^S1][^S4] Most of the "boost" list fails: the pomegranate "+30%" is an **unrefereed conference abstract**,[^S42] "seed oils crash testosterone" rests on a **handful of small, old studies**,[^S31] "sunning the testicles raised T 200%" is a **flawed 1939 study**,[^S53] **soy lowering testosterone is a myth** (three meta-analyses),[^S36][^S96] and the popular **"cut masturbation to raise T"** advice rests on a study **RETRACTED in 2021**.[^S43] The best-evidenced supplement is **ashwagandha**, and even it is modest.[^S22] No natural protocol reaches the "900–1000 ng/dL" targets these guides set; that needs exogenous testosterone.

## Tier 1 — The strongest evidence (many studies agree)

### Supplements do almost nothing in a healthy man — the deficiency-correction rule

This is the single most-replicated pattern in the whole field. For every micronutrient the story repeats: **fix a real deficiency and testosterone recovers; give the same nutrient to a replete man and nothing happens.** Vitamin D is the clearest case — the "+25%" that started the hype came from a small secondary analysis of overweight men who were *also losing weight*,[^S13] and it collapses under control: a well-powered trial (n=98) was null,[^S14] a 17-trial meta-analysis found a trivial +0.38 nmol/L,[^S15] two further trials — including one in *low-testosterone* men — were null,[^S74][^S75] and the positive observational link is driven almost entirely by frail, unwell men.[^S73] Zinc restriction crashes testosterone and repletion restores it,[^S16][^S17] but high-dose zinc in men who already have enough does nothing.[^S18] A systematic review of 52 studies across 27 ingredients concludes most "boosters" simply fail.[^S21] Test before you supplement.

### The acute training / sex / competition "spike" is inert

The "compound lifts spike your testosterone" claim is true and irrelevant. A landmark within-subject study deliberately triggered big post-workout testosterone/growth-hormone surges and got **identical** muscle and strength gains versus a low-hormone arm;[^S11] a larger cohort found no link between the size of the spike and the size of the gains.[^S12] The same holds for sex — orgasm itself doesn't change testosterone,[^S44] a three-week abstinence nudges *resting* baseline only slightly,[^S45] and social/sexual context (a woman's company, a sex club at +72%, winning a competition) raises it **acutely and transiently only**.[^S48] None of it durably moves your baseline. Train for strength, fat loss and health.

### Body fat is the biggest lever — and the causality is proven

This is the strongest actionable finding, and it vindicates "cut the fat" for the right reason. Weight loss reliably raises testosterone (24-study meta-analysis: diet ~+2.9, bariatric surgery ~+8.7 nmol/L),[^S7] with EMAS showing the same longitudinally.[^S6] The keystone is a **bidirectional Mendelian-randomization study**: a genetically-driven rise in BMI *lowers* testosterone while the reverse isn't seen, so causality runs **BMI → testosterone**, with a shift from BMI 30 to 25 worth about **+13%**.[^S95] So the mechanism behind "cut sugar/carbs" is **fat loss**, not carbohydrates. One caveat: the popular "belly fat aromatizes testosterone into estrogen" story is only partly right — the main lesion in obese men is central (pituitary/SHBG), not aromatization.[^S5]

### The population decline is real and cross-national — but the cause isn't settled

Age-matched testosterone fell in the US,[^S1] with independent replication in Danish[^S2] and Finnish[^S3] surveys and an extension into young men that persists after adjusting for body weight.[^S94] It is **not** "just obesity": adjusting for BMI removed the trend in the Danish data[^S2] but not the Finnish,[^S3] and US veterans who stayed weight-stable still lost ~19% of their testosterone over 20 years.[^S4] It sits alongside a ~51.6% global sperm-count decline since the 1970s,[^S89] part of the broader testicular-dysgenesis pattern tied to fetal anti-androgen exposure.[^S90] Directionally the guides are right; their single-cause explanations are not.

### Soy does not lower testosterone or feminize men — a myth killed three times

Fifteen controlled groups,[^S35] then an expanded 41-study analysis (up to n=1,753),[^S36] then a **new 2025 dose-response meta-analysis**[^S96] all found no effect of soy or isoflavones on total testosterone, free testosterone, estradiol or SHBG at any dose. The "eliminate soy" advice is a myth for hormones.

### Phthalates are the best-supported everyday anti-androgen — but modest and age-specific

Of the "plastics lower your testosterone" claims, phthalates (especially DEHP) are the one with real human evidence. A 28,911-participant meta-analysis ties DEHP to lower testosterone and free-androgen index;[^S50] NHANES shows the effect is age-specific (a doubling of DEHP → ~−7.7% testosterone in men over 60);[^S51] a US-EPA systematic review confirms DEHP and DBP as human anti-androgens;[^S85] an occupational study saw testosterone fall *within workers* as a metabolite rose.[^S86] Reducing plastic food-contact (glass/metal/ceramic) is a reasonable, modest lever — not a rescue.

## Tier 2 — Well-supported, with real nuance

### Sleep: the direction is real, the magnitude isn't

The famous "−15%" comes from one n=10 study;[^S9] two later randomized trials (n=14 and n=13) found a **clean null**.[^S10] A meta-analysis reconciles it: only *total* sleep deprivation clearly lowers testosterone, while *partial* restriction is not significant.[^S64] And the sleep-apnea↔low-testosterone link is largely body fat — CPAP doesn't raise testosterone,[^S66] and the association disappears after adjusting for adiposity.[^S65][^S67] Protect sleep; treat the percentage as uncertain.

### Stress and energy: chronic lowers T, acute can raise it, under-fuelling backfires

*Acute* stress can briefly *raise* testosterone while *prolonged* stress lowers it, recovering afterward,[^S60] and the suppression is a central, reversible adaptation, not testicular failure.[^S61] Fuel matters too: calorie restriction raises testosterone in overweight men but *lowers* it in lean men,[^S62] severe under-fuelling drives functional hypogonadism (the male athlete triad),[^S63] and chronic high-volume **endurance** overtraining lowers resting testosterone.[^S68] "Manage stress, don't under-fuel, don't over-train" is right — a short stressor isn't the problem.

### Alcohol, sugar, and diet composition

Heavy/chronic drinking reliably lowers testosterone (meta-analysis of 10,199 men);[^S39] a small acute dose can briefly *raise* it. A sugar load acutely drops testosterone ~25% for about two hours,[^S57] confirmed with a water control,[^S58] but it's a transient post-meal dip — the lasting harm from sugar is via body fat. On diet composition: very-low-fat diets modestly lower testosterone,[^S32] and while carb restriction itself is roughly neutral, **very-high-protein (≥35%) low-carb diets sharply lower** it[^S33] (and the "keto raises testosterone" signal is confounded by concurrent weight loss[^S34]). Adequate fat matters; extremes backfire.

### Ashwagandha is the best-evidenced supplement here — modest, industry-heavy

Not in either source guide, yet the strongest option on the shelf. A 2026 meta-analysis (23 trials, n=1,706) found a men-only rise of ~+57 ng/dL tied to lowering cortisol;[^S22] individual trials show +14.7%[^S23] and a significant +96 ng/dL,[^S24] though several are manufacturer-funded, and a herbs review found only 9 of 32 trials showed any rise.[^S79] Real but modest, largest in stressed/overweight men.

### "900–1000 ng/dL" is at or above the healthy ceiling; "low T" is a clinical diagnosis

Harmonized reference ranges put healthy non-obese young men at **264–916 ng/dL**,[^S91] so 900 is near the very top and **1000 is above** the healthy young-adult range — not a dial you turn naturally. Clinically, "low testosterone" is a symptoms-plus-two-morning-tests diagnosis, not a number to chase.[^S92] The only thing that reaches supraphysiological levels is exogenous testosterone — which does so by shutting down your own production, causing testicular atrophy and infertility.[^S93]

### BPA: real but modest — a hormone-balance shift, not a "crash"

A meta-analysis links BPA to higher SHBG/estradiol, lower bioavailable androgen and lower sperm;[^S49] an independent clinic cohort found BPA associated with a *lower* free-androgen index and higher FSH — impaired testicular function, not a total-testosterone collapse.[^S87] The "plastics crash your testosterone" verb overstates a modest effect. (The BPA exposure route is covered in the separate [BPA in thermal receipts](bpa-thermal-receipts.md) research.)

## Tier 3 — Weak, single-study, or conditional

**Herbs beyond ashwagandha.** Only **tongkat ali** has decent evidence (strongest in hypogonadal men);[^S25] **tribulus** is a confirmed null;[^S28] **D-aspartic acid** is null-to-negative in trained men (6 g even *lowered* testosterone);[^S29][^S30] fenugreek and maca reliably raise libido while leaving testosterone within range.[^S82]

**Creatine and hair loss.** Creatine is the one supplement the testosterone crowd genuinely should take, and the hair-loss fear rests on a single 2009 study that raised the DHT ratio but *never measured hair*.[^S69] The first trial to actually measure hair outcomes (2025) found no effect,[^S70] and the consensus review agrees creatine doesn't raise testosterone/DHT or cause hair loss.[^S71]

**Odd singletons.** Smoking is paradoxically linked to *higher* testosterone (an association, not advice);[^S72] boron rests on one open-label n=8 study;[^S76] organ-meat eaters had *lower* sperm motility and no testosterone benefit — the viral "52% higher sperm count" is blog-sourced;[^S40] and vitamin A helps only boys with subnormal intake.[^S41]

## Tier 4 — Debunked myths / discredited single sources

**"Cut masturbation to raise T" rests on a retracted study.** The scientific anchor for "abstinence boosts testosterone" is Jiang 2003 ("7-day abstinence → testosterone peaks at 145.7%"), which was **RETRACTED in 2021**.[^S43] Setting the retraction aside, it was a transient one-day spike in n=28, never replicated. The controlled evidence says orgasm doesn't change testosterone,[^S44] a three-week abstinence produces only a modest higher *resting* baseline,[^S45] and habitual frequency doesn't alter baseline. The "each ejaculation depletes zinc" mechanism fails the arithmetic (~1 mg zinc per ejaculate, diet-replaceable). "Sperm tokens" has no endocrine basis.

**Pomegranate "+30% in 40 days" is an unrefereed conference abstract** — uncontrolled, mixed-sex (22 men / 38 women), using salivary testosterone, reporting ~+24%, never peer-reviewed or replicated.[^S42]

**"Sun on testicles +200%" is a single flawed 1939 study** — institutionalized psychiatric men on electroconvulsive therapy, testosterone measured as a urinary metabolite (not serum), uncontrolled, never replicated in 85 years.[^S53]

**"Seed oils crash testosterone"** rests on a handful of small, old correlations (Volek 1997, n=12, and similar),[^S31] with no adequately-powered modern trial isolating fat *type* at matched total fat. Fat *quantity* is a separate, supported point (very-low-fat lowers T[^S32]); the seed-oil-specific claim is unproven. And the "credit card of microplastic a week" is the extreme upper bound of a modelling estimate that has no testosterone endpoint at all[^S56] — any hormonal risk is the leached chemicals, not the plastic mass. (Organophosphate pesticides, tellingly, damage sperm *without* lowering testosterone.[^S88])

## What this rebuild changed

This page was rebuilt on 14 July 2026 by re-reading **all 96 cited studies one-by-one from their source abstracts** and re-verifying every identifier against PubMed. That process **confirmed the headline and every myth-debunk above**, and it corrected a few errors in the earlier version — in the spirit of showing the work: two citations had the *wrong* study ID attached (now fixed), one endocrine-disruptor finding had its direction reversed (BPA lowers bioavailable androgen — it does not raise testosterone), and two new sources were added: a Mendelian-randomization study proving the body-fat→testosterone causal link, and a 2025 soy meta-analysis. Reading from source did not overturn the conclusion; it hardened it.

## The honest natural-testosterone checklist

1. **Get to a healthy body-fat level** — the single biggest lever, causally proven.[^S95][^S7]
2. **Protect sleep** — direction real, worth it.[^S9]
3. **Correct a real vitamin-D or zinc deficiency** — test first; don't megadose if replete.[^S14][^S18]
4. **Keep heavy alcohol down**,[^S39] and **don't chronically under-fuel** if you're lean.[^S62][^S63]
5. **Train for strength and health**, not for a hormone spike;[^S11] take **creatine** (safe, doesn't cause hair loss).[^S70]
6. **Reduce plastic food-contact modestly** — glass/metal/ceramic.[^S50]
7. Optional small add: **ashwagandha**, especially if stressed.[^S22]

Everything else — pomegranate protocols, no-fap, seed-oil avoidance, sun-on-testicles, zinc/magnesium megadoses in replete men, soy fear — is small, conditional, or a myth. This is informational, not medical advice; measured low testosterone with symptoms is a doctor's evaluation, not a supplement problem.
