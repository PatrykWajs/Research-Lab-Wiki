# Testosterone: What Actually Raises It vs What People Think

> Men's-health seminars and carnivore-diet guides promise to "boost testosterone naturally" with a long list of foods, supplements, no-fap, sun exposure and lifestyle rules. Which of those actually work, how strong is the evidence, and which are myths?

**Complete** · Published 10 July 2026 · *Updated 11 July 2026 (+38 sources)* · **94 cited studies** · Confidence: **High** on the "restore-not-boost" framing and the debunked myths · **Moderate** on the exact magnitudes (sleep, ashwagandha).

!!! abstract "TL;DR"
    For a healthy man, "natural testosterone optimization" is mostly **deficiency-correction and body composition, not boosting**. Three levers do almost all the real work: **lose excess body fat** (the single biggest one),[^S7] **protect sleep**,[^S9] and **don't be deficient** in vitamin D or zinc — correcting a real deficiency helps, supplementing an already-healthy man does nothing.[^S14][^S18] The population decline that these guides cite is **real** but its cause is unresolved.[^S1][^S4] Most of the popular "boost" list fails: the acute training/sex testosterone spike is **inert** for building muscle,[^S11] vitamin-D/zinc/magnesium/selenium only help in deficiency, the pomegranate "+30%" traces to an **unrefereed conference abstract**,[^S42] "seed oils crash testosterone" rests on one **n=12 study from 1997**,[^S31] "sunning the testicles raised T 200%" is a **flawed 1939 study**,[^S53] **soy lowering testosterone is a myth**,[^S36] and the popular **"cut masturbation to raise T"** advice rests on a study that was **RETRACTED in 2021**.[^S43] Endocrine disruptors (phthalates, BPA) are real but modest — and it's the leached chemicals, not the plastic mass, that matter.[^S49][^S51] The best-evidenced supplement is **ashwagandha**, and even it is modest and industry-heavy.[^S22] No natural protocol reaches the "900-1000 ng/dL" targets these guides set; that needs exogenous testosterone.

!!! tip "Key Takeaways"
    - **Restore, don't boost.** You can recover testosterone a fixable problem is suppressing (fat, sleep, deficiency, heavy drinking); once those are handled the dial mostly stops turning.[^S14][^S18]
    - **Body fat is the biggest lever.** Obese men run ~30% lower testosterone; weight loss reliably raises it (diet ~+2.9, bariatric surgery ~+8.7 nmol/L).[^S7][^S8]
    - **The "belly fat → estrogen" aromatase story is only partly right** — the main lesion in obesity is central (pituitary), not aromatization.[^S5]
    - **Sleep matters, but the famous "−15%" is one n=10 study** that two later trials did not cleanly reproduce.[^S9][^S10]
    - **The post-workout testosterone spike does not build muscle** — triggering big hormone surges gave identical gains vs a low-hormone arm.[^S11]
    - **Supplements only fix deficiencies.** The strongest vitamin-D RCT (n=98) and high-dose zinc in replete men are both null.[^S14][^S18]
    - **Soy does not lower testosterone or feminize men** — settled by a 41-study meta-analysis.[^S36]
    - **"Cut masturbation to raise T"** rests on the **retracted** Jiang 2003; ejaculation does not chronically lower testosterone.[^S43][^S45]
    - **Ashwagandha** is the one supplement with decent evidence — a modest, cortisol-mediated, men-only bump.[^S22]
    - **Anabolic steroids** are the only thing that raises testosterone fast — by replacing your own production and shutting it down, with real harms.

## 1. "Boost" vs "restore" — the honest framing

The popular genre promises to *boost* testosterone. The evidence supports a narrower claim: you can *restore* testosterone that a fixable problem is suppressing — obesity, poor sleep, a genuine micronutrient deficiency, heavy drinking, illness. Once those are handled, the dial mostly stops turning. Across every nutrient and herb studied, correcting a deficiency helps and supplementing an already-replete man does little or nothing.[^S14][^S18][^S15] That is why the "900-1000 ng/dL" targets these guides set are aspirational: normal ranges run ~250-900 ng/dL, and reaching the top end naturally is a body-composition-and-sleep story, not a supplement stack. Chasing higher reliably needs exogenous androgens — which raise testosterone fast but shut down natural production and carry real cardiovascular and fertility harms.

## 2. The population decline is real — the cause isn't settled

Both source guides open on the decline, and they are directionally right. Age-matched male testosterone fell ~1.2%/yr in the US MMAS cohort (a 65-year-old in 2004 ran ~15% below a 65-year-old in 1987),[^S1] with independent replication in Danish and Finnish population surveys — a genuine, cross-national, multi-decade drop. What neither gets right is the **cause**, because nobody has settled it. Rising obesity explains part of it, but not all — a US veteran cohort found men who stayed weight-stable still lost ~19% of their testosterone over 20 years,[^S4] and the decline extends into adolescents and young men, persisting after adjusting for body weight.[^S94] The honest read is multifactorial and partly unexplained; the endocrine-disruptor story (§8) is a plausible contributor, not a proven primary driver.

## 3. Body fat — the biggest real lever

This is the strongest finding in the whole area, and it validates the "cut the fat, cut refined carbs" instinct for the right reason. Obese men run ~30% lower total testosterone than lean men;[^S8] a 24-study meta-analysis found weight loss raised it (diet ~+2.9, bariatric surgery ~+8.7 nmol/L),[^S7] and Mendelian-randomization confirms higher BMI *causes* lower testosterone. So the mechanism behind "cut sugar/carbs" is **fat loss**, not carbohydrates themselves. Two caveats: the popular "belly fat aromatizes testosterone into estrogen" mechanism is only partly right — the primary lesion in obese men is central (secondary hypogonadism),[^S5] and "lifting raises testosterone more than losing fat" is not supported.

## 4. Diet: what survives, what's folklore

**Fat quantity matters; "eat cholesterol to boost T" does not.** Very-low-fat diets modestly lower testosterone (SMD −0.38 across 6 intervention studies),[^S32] so crash-low-fat eating is counterproductive. But cholesterol is not the rate-limiting step in testosterone synthesis, and no trial shows eating more cholesterol raises it in a healthy man — the "steak and eggs give the precursor" logic is a substrate fallacy.[^S31]

**"Seed oils crash testosterone"** rests almost entirely on one 1997 study — n=12, cross-sectional correlations (polyunsaturated-to-saturated fat ratio r≈−0.63), not a causal experiment.[^S31] No adequately powered trial isolates saturated vs polyunsaturated fat at matched total fat. Fat *type* is a plausible but unproven hypothesis; the certainty outruns the data.

**"Low-carb raises testosterone"** is overstated. A 27-study meta-analysis found moderate-protein low-carb diets neutral, while high-protein (≥35% of calories) low-carb diets *lowered* resting testosterone ~−5.2 nmol/L;[^S33] the "keto raises T" studies are confounded by concurrent weight loss. Carb restriction helps only insofar as it drives fat loss, and pushing protein very high backfires.

**Soy does not lower testosterone or feminize men.** Two meta-analyses — 15 controlled groups, then an expanded 41-study analysis (up to n=1,753) — found no effect of soy or isoflavones on total testosterone, free testosterone, estradiol or SHBG.[^S36] The "eliminate soy" advice is a myth for hormones.

**Organ meats, liver, testicles.** The one peer-reviewed study of organ-meat intake vs reproductive hormones found *lower* sperm motility and no testosterone effect;[^S40] the viral "52% higher sperm count" figure is blog-sourced, not from any published organ-meat trial. Eating testicles has no human evidence at all.

**Alcohol.** Heavy/chronic drinking reliably suppresses the gonadal axis;[^S39] acutely, a small dose can briefly raise testosterone and intoxication lowers it. "Limit alcohol" is right for heavy use; "any drink tanks T" overstates it.

## 5. Sleep, training, sun

**Sleep** is worth protecting, but the specific "−15%" number comes from one n=10 study[^S9] that two later randomized trials did not cleanly reproduce.[^S10] Treat the magnitude as uncertain, the direction as real. **Resistance training**: the "compound lifts give the biggest testosterone spike" claim is true and irrelevant — a landmark study showed that triggering big post-workout hormone surges produced *identical* muscle and strength gains vs a low-hormone arm,[^S11] and chronic training barely moves *resting* testosterone. Train for strength, fat loss and health, not for a hormone spike. **"Sunning the testicles raised T 200%"** traces to a 1939 study on institutionalized psychiatric men on electroconvulsive therapy, with testosterone measured as urinary metabolites, uncontrolled, and never properly replicated.[^S53] Sensible sun helps vitamin D; the genital-tanning number is a wellness amplification of one 87-year-old outlier.

## 6. Supplements: the deficiency-correction rule (and the one that half-works)

The pattern is identical for every micronutrient: **fix a deficiency and testosterone recovers; supplement a replete man and nothing happens.**

- **Vitamin D:** the "+25%" origin study was small, secondary, and confounded by weight loss;[^S13] the strongest RCT (n=98) was null[^S14] and a 17-RCT meta-analysis found a trivial ~0.4 nmol/L effect;[^S15] further RCTs — including one in men with *low* testosterone — were also null,[^S75] and the positive observational link is driven by frail/comorbid men, not healthy ones.[^S73]
- **Zinc:** restriction crashes testosterone and repletion restores it,[^S16] but high-dose zinc in zinc-sufficient men does nothing.[^S18] Permissive, not ergogenic.
- **Magnesium / selenium:** the evidence is one small confounded study and infertile-men-only data under a 2023 Expression of Concern — neither is a demonstrated booster.

The exception is **ashwagandha** — in neither source guide, yet the best-evidenced option here. A 2026 meta-analysis (23 RCTs, n=1,706) found a men-only rise of ~+57 ng/dL, tied to lowering cortisol;[^S22] the most-cited RCT (+14.7%) was funded by the extract maker,[^S23] and a herbs-and-testosterone systematic review found only 9 of 32 trials showed any significant rise.[^S79] Real but modest, largest in stressed/overweight men. Among other herbs, only **tongkat ali** has decent evidence (strongest in hypogonadal men);[^S25] **tribulus** is a confirmed null,[^S28] and **D-aspartic acid** is null-to-negative in trained men (6 g even lowered testosterone).[^S30] A 52-study umbrella review sums it up: most "test boosters" fail to raise testosterone.[^S21]

**Pomegranate** deserves its own line because the "+30% in 40 days" claim is so specific. That number traces to a *conference abstract* (never peer-reviewed), uncontrolled, mixed-sex (22 men / 38 women), using salivary testosterone, reporting ~+24%.[^S42] Cheap and low-risk — but not solid evidence.

## 7. The masturbation myth

A whole strand of men's advice is built on cutting masturbation: "each ejaculation depletes zinc/magnesium," "sperm tokens," the prostate has "finite uses." The scientific anchor for the underlying "abstinence boosts testosterone" belief is Jiang 2003 ("7-day abstinence → testosterone peaks at 145.7%") — which was **RETRACTED in 2021** for duplication.[^S43] Setting the retraction aside, it was a transient one-day spike in n=28, never replicated, with no functional benefit. The controlled evidence says the opposite: orgasm itself does not change testosterone,[^S44] a 3-week abstinence produces only a modest higher *resting* baseline,[^S45] and no study shows habitual ejaculation frequency (weekly to daily) alters baseline testosterone. The "mineral depletion" mechanism also fails the arithmetic — an ejaculate holds on the order of ~1 mg of zinc, a small diet-replaceable fraction of daily intake. Partnered sex, novelty and competition all raise testosterone — but **acutely and transiently only**,[^S48] with no durable baseline change.

## 8. Endocrine disruptors — real, modest, and it's the chemicals not the plastic mass

Both guides are directionally right that plastics and chemicals matter, and both overstate the magnitude. The best-supported everyday anti-androgen is **phthalates (DEHP)**: consistent human data across NHANES cycles tie DEHP to lower testosterone and free-androgen index (a doubling → ~−7.7% testosterone in older men),[^S51] a US-EPA systematic review confirms DEHP and DBP as human anti-androgens,[^S85] an occupational study saw testosterone fall *within workers* as a phthalate metabolite rose,[^S86] and the founding human signal was reduced anogenital distance in phthalate-exposed male infants.[^S84] **BPA**'s effect is real but modest and is an androgen-to-estrogen shift (higher SHBG/estradiol, lower free androgen, lower sperm), not a total-testosterone "crash"[^S49] — one clinic cohort even found BPA linked to *higher* T, LH and estradiol, i.e. axis disruption rather than simple suppression.[^S87] **Atrazine**'s dramatic feminization is a frog result[^S52] with weak human evidence; organophosphate pesticides, tellingly, damage sperm *without* lowering testosterone;[^S88] and the **"credit card of microplastic a week"** is the extreme upper bound of a modelling range shown to overestimate by orders of magnitude, with no proven link between plastic particles and testosterone[^S56] — the hormonal risk is the leached chemicals, not the plastic mass. (The BPA exposure route is covered in depth in the separate [BPA in thermal receipts](bpa-thermal-receipts.md) research.) Net: reducing plastic food-contact is a reasonable modest lever, not a testosterone rescue.

## 10. The honest natural-testosterone checklist

1. **Get to a healthy body-fat level** — the single biggest lever.[^S7]
2. **Protect sleep** — direction real, worth it.[^S9]
3. **Correct a real vitamin-D or zinc deficiency** — test first; don't megadose if replete.[^S14][^S18]
4. **Keep heavy alcohol down.**[^S39]
5. **Train for strength and health**, not for a hormone spike.[^S11]
6. **Reduce plastic food-contact modestly** — glass/metal/ceramic.[^S51]
7. Optional small add: **ashwagandha**, especially if stressed.[^S22]

Everything else — pomegranate protocols, no-fap, seed-oil avoidance, sun-on-testicles, zinc/magnesium megadoses in replete men, soy fear — is small, unproven, or a myth. This is informational, not medical advice; measured low testosterone with symptoms is a doctor's evaluation, not a supplement problem.

## 11. What a second research pass added

A follow-up pass added 38 more verified studies and sharpened several points:

- **Sugar has a mechanism now:** a 75-g glucose load acutely drops total testosterone ~25% for about two hours,[^S57] confirmed in a water-controlled crossover,[^S58] but it's a transient post-meal dip — the lasting harm is via body fat. And a **calorie deficit only raises testosterone if you're carrying excess fat**; in lean men, under-eating can *lower* it,[^S62] and severe low energy availability drives functional hypogonadism (the male athlete triad).[^S63]
- **Chronic endurance overtraining lowers resting testosterone** (the "exercise-hypogonadal male condition"),[^S68] a real caveat to "exercise raises T" — it's high-volume energy deficit, not intensity.
- **Sleep, more precisely:** only *total* sleep deprivation clearly lowers testosterone; partial restriction is inconsistent,[^S64] and the sleep-apnea link is largely explained by body fat — CPAP does not raise testosterone.[^S66]
- **Creatine and hair loss:** creatine is the one supplement the testosterone crowd genuinely should take, and the hair-loss fear rests on a single 2009 study that raised the DHT ratio but *never measured hair*.[^S69] The first trial to actually measure hair outcomes (2025) found no effect,[^S70] and the consensus review concludes creatine does not raise testosterone or DHT or cause hair loss.[^S71]
- **More "boosters" fall:** maca reliably helps libido but does **not** change testosterone;[^S82] boron and omega-3 don't raise it in healthy men; shilajit rests on a single small trial;[^S81] and vitamin D was null even in a trial of men with *low* testosterone.[^S74] (Counterintuitively, smoking is associated with *higher* testosterone[^S72] — an association, not advice.)
- **Anabolic steroids** reach high numbers only by shutting down your own production, causing testicular atrophy and infertility.[^S93] "Normal" itself is wide: harmonized data put healthy young men at **264–916 ng/dL**,[^S91] so a "900–1000" target is high-normal, not a level to force — and clinically, "low testosterone" is a symptoms-plus-two-morning-tests diagnosis, not a number to chase.[^S92]
- **The decline in context:** the population testosterone drop extends into young men, alongside a **~50% decline in sperm counts** since the 1970s[^S89] — part of the broader testicular-dysgenesis pattern tied to fetal anti-androgen exposure,[^S90] the reproductive-health backdrop to the endocrine-disruptor concern.

None of it changes the headline: **lose fat, protect sleep, fix real deficiencies.** The rest is small, conditional, or myth.
