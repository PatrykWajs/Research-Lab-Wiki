    Creatine: What It Actually Does vs What People Think¶

    Creatine is the most-studied and best-selling sports supplement on earth — and the most myth-laden. It is blamed for kidney damage, hair loss, "being a steroid," permanent bloating and cramps, and sold in a dozen "advanced" forms. What does it actually do, which claims (good and bad) survive the peer-reviewed evidence, and how should a healthy person use it? This is the whole picture, ranked by how strongly the evidence backs each claim — settled consensus first, single-study and myth last.

    TL;DR

    Creatine monohydrate is the most-studied, best-evidenced and safest sports supplement, and almost every scary claim about it is wrong. Taken daily and paired with resistance training it genuinely builds strength and lean mass,12 though the fast early gain is partly intracellular water1312 and it does almost nothing for muscle without training.1 The "it wrecks your kidneys" fear is a measurement artifact — creatine raises serum creatinine without lowering true kidney filtration1416 — and the scare traces to two confounded case reports.2223 It does not cause dehydration or cramps (if anything, fewer),3032 it is not a steroid and does not change testosterone,34 and the hair-loss panic rests on one 2009 study that never measured hair36 — the only trial that did measure hair was null.38 You do not need to load or cycle it,39 and plain monohydrate beats every "advanced" form.41424344 Brain, mood and metabolic benefits are real but bounded — largest in older adults, vegetarians, the sleep-deprived, or as an antidepressant adjunct in women,464760 and small-to-null in young, rested people.51 The honest caveat cutting the other way: much of the enthusiastic literature is industry-funded — but the safety core is independently corroborated.14285161

    What this review covered¶

    An eight-part search swept the peer-reviewed literature across the ergogenic core, kidney safety, hormones and hair, brain and mood, women/aging/bone, mechanism and product forms, metabolic and therapeutic claims, and the specific influencer myths (steroid, cycling, bloating, teenagers, vegetarians, contamination). Every candidate PMID — 119 — was then independently title-matched against PubMed (0 wrong, 0 retracted), and the load-bearing abstracts (the efficacy meta-analyses, the kidney meta, the DHT and hair trials, the depression and glycemic RCTs, the metabolic-burden paper, the postmenopausal and cachexia trials) were read from source for their exact numbers. A recurring honesty note runs through it: much of the positive creatine research comes from the industry-linked International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN), which is flagged throughout — but the safety core is backed by independent groups.

    Tier 1 — The strongest evidence (it works, and the safety scares are wrong)¶

    It builds muscle and strength — but the training does most of the work¶

    This is the most-replicated result in sports supplementation. Meta-analyses agree that creatine combined with resistance training builds lean mass and strength: a 35-trial meta put the lean-mass gain at +1.10 kg with training (and a non-significant +0.03 kg without exercise),1 a 22-trial meta in older adults found +1.37 kg lean mass plus greater chest- and leg-press strength,23 and strength meta-analyses (upper and lower limb) agree.456 The load-bearing nuance is size and dependency: the pooled body-composition effect is "small-to-moderate,"7 and creatine is an amplifier of resistance training, not a substitute for it — with no training stimulus it does almost nothing.1

    It is real muscle — but the fast early gain is partly water¶

    The "it's just water weight" line is half true. Muscle-biopsy trials show creatine drives genuine growth: larger fiber cross-sectional area,89 more satellite cells and myonuclei (true remodeling),10 and an anabolic gene-expression signature.11 But the rapid first-week increase in scale weight is largely intracellular water drawn into the muscle cell,12 and a 2025 RCT with a supplement wash-in showed that once that water was accounted for, 5 g/day added no lean mass beyond training alone (a +0.51 kg wash-in bump, then no between-group difference).13 So the honest picture is real hypertrophy over months, with the "instant gains" of the first week being cell hydration.

    The kidney scare is a measurement artifact¶

    Creatine is metabolized to creatinine, so supplementing mildly raises serum creatinine — and therefore lowers estimated-GFR equations — without any true fall in kidney filtration.1415 Studies using measured GFR (⁵¹Cr-EDTA and clearance markers) find no renal impairment,1617 including in resistance-trained men on high-protein diets18 and in type-2 diabetics with elevated renal risk;19 three separate meta-analyses reach the same conclusion.142021 The entire "creatine destroys kidneys" narrative traces to two confounded single-patient case reports — both in people with pre-existing kidney disease and on other nephrotoxic drugs.2223 A 2023 review put it bluntly: it is time for a "requiem" for the creatine-kidney-failure myth.24

    It is safe long-term, across populations¶

    Up to 21 months of use left roughly 50 clinical health markers unchanged;25 the ISSN safe ceiling is 30 g/day for five years;26 and high doses were tolerated in months-long trials in ALS27 patients. A dedicated female-only adverse-event meta-analysis (29 studies / 951 women) recorded no deaths and no serious adverse events, and no difference versus placebo in total adverse events, gastrointestinal events, weight gain, or renal/hepatic markers.28 Youth are not a special-risk group either — a review of active adolescents found a strong safety profile and no evidence-based reason for a blanket ban.29

    No dehydration, no cramps — if anything, fewer¶

    The dehydration/cramp myth runs the opposite way to the evidence. A meta-analysis found no impairment of hydration or thermoregulation and no rise in heat-illness risk;30 subjects who exercised dehydrated in the heat were unharmed;31 and season-long football cohorts had fewer cramps, dehydration episodes and injuries on creatine.3233 The intracellular water creatine draws into muscle is, if anything, protective.

    It is not a steroid and does not raise testosterone¶

    Creatine is a naturally occurring amino-acid derivative found in meat and fish — not a hormone, and not a banned substance. Controlled trials show no change versus placebo in total testosterone, SHBG, free-androgen index, cortisol, growth hormone or IGF-1,34 and umbrella reviews of "testosterone boosters" place it outside the category of supplements that raise serum T.35

    Tier 2 — Well-supported, with real nuance (hair, dosing, forms, brain)¶

    The hair-loss scare rests on one study that never measured hair¶

    The entire "creatine causes baldness" claim comes from a single 2009 study in 20 rugby players, in which DHT rose (+56% after loading, +40% after maintenance) and the DHT-to-testosterone ratio rose — but which never measured hair and was never replicated,36 and was formally critiqued at publication.37 In 2025 the first trial designed to look at hair directly — 12 weeks, 5 g/day, with Trichogram and FotoFinder imaging — found no change in DHT, in the DHT:T ratio, or in any hair-density, thickness or follicle-count measure versus placebo, and concluded it was "strong evidence against the claim that creatine contributes to hair loss."38 The mechanism is an unreplicated hypothesis; the only outcome data are null.

    You do not need to "load," and you do not need to "cycle"¶

    A loading phase (20 g/day for about six days) saturates muscle creatine quickly, but 3–5 g/day reaches the identical saturation in three to four weeks — loading only speeds arrival, it is not required.3940 There is likewise no cycling requirement: continuous 3–5 g/day simply maintains the elevated stores, and stopping lets them drift back to baseline over about a month.39

    Plain monohydrate is the gold standard — the "advanced" forms are marketing¶

    Creatine monohydrate is roughly 99% bioavailable and raises muscle stores 15–40%.41 Head-to-head trials show every marketed alternative fails to beat it: creatine ethyl ester was actually worse (lower muscle-creatine gain, more degradation),42 buffered "Kre-Alkalyn" was no better even at a lower dose,43 and creatine nitrate did not outperform equimolar monohydrate.44 Pay for monohydrate, ideally a tested brand.

    Brain benefits are real but concentrated in deficit and stress¶

    Creatine crosses the blood–brain barrier slowly — brain creatine rises only about 8% versus roughly 20% in muscle45 — so the cognitive effect is largest where the brain is short of it or under stress: memory in older adults,46 vegetarians (lower baseline stores),4748 and sleep deprivation.4950 In young, rested, meat-eating adults the largest trial to date (123 people) found only a small, borderline effect and no vegetarian advantage,51 and a balanced review confirms the benefit shrinks toward zero without a deficit.50 The "response" question is the same story from the muscle side: uptake is inversely related to your baseline, so low-store individuals — especially vegetarians — gain the most and already-saturated meat-eaters the least.5253

    Women benefit and it is safe¶

    Women carry roughly 70–80% lower endogenous creatine stores than men.54 The evidence in women is favorable: a 2026 meta-analysis of postmenopausal trials found +0.37 kg lean mass and +7.5 kg leg-press strength when at least 5 g/day is combined with resistance training,55 vulnerable older women improved lean mass and function with creatine plus training,56 and there is no evidence that creatine disrupts female sex hormones.57

    Tier 3 — Promising but preliminary, or conditional¶

    • Depression — the strongest data are for augmentation: adding 5 g/day to an SSRI accelerated and deepened the response in women with major depression (greater symptom improvement from week 2),60 with a supporting brain-bioenergetics rationale62 and an association in a large dietary survey.63 But this is adjunct-to-medication in women, not a monotherapy, and an open-label study saw both bipolar patients switch into hypomania — a real safety flag.64
    • Blood sugar — creatine plus exercise cut HbA1c by 1.1% in type-2 diabetics and raised GLUT-4 translocation (the strongest human trial),65 but without an exercise co-intervention, meta-analyses find no glycemic effect.6667
    • Fatigue / long-COVID — small, mostly single-blind trials suggest 6 g/day reduces fatigue and raises grip strength;6869 promising, not established.
    • Bone — the best 12-month trial attenuated femoral-neck bone-density loss and improved hip bone geometry with resistance training,70 but overall bone density is unchanged in the meta-analysis55 and reviews decline to recommend creatine as an osteoporosis therapy.7172
    • Immune / inflammation — an acute blunting of post-exertion cytokines,73 but no chronic anti-inflammatory effect (a meta of double-blind trials was null for CRP, IL-6 and TNF).74
    • Brain injury — mechanistically promising, with one small pediatric study,75 but no adult concussion trial.50
    • Caffeine — the "caffeine cancels creatine" rule is a single 1996 study of nine people;76 later reviews find everyday co-use is fine.7778

    Tier 4 — Myths, misconceptions, and marketing¶

    • "It's a steroid / a banned PED." No — it is an amino-acid derivative from meat and fish, not a hormone, and it is not on any banned list.3526
    • "It wrecks your kidneys." The higher creatinine reading is the metabolic artifact, not lost filtration; measured-GFR studies and meta-analyses show no decline even in kidney patients.1724
    • "Water bloat you can't lose." The water it retains is inside the muscle cell, not subcutaneous, so it does not read as a puffy look, and it reverses within about four weeks of stopping.1239
    • "You must cycle it." There is no physiological basis; continuous 3–5 g/day simply keeps stores saturated.39
    • "It causes hair loss." One DHT study, no hair endpoint, direct hair trial null (see Tier 2).3638
    • "Bad for teenagers." No evidence of harm in active youth.29
    • "Vegetarians don't need it." Backwards — they have lower baseline stores and respond the most.947
    • Cardiac and cancer-cachexia cures. Creatine improved skeletal-muscle endurance but not heart function in heart-failure trials,79 added nothing to cardiac rehabilitation,80 and a 263-patient cancer trial found no benefit for the anorexia/weight-loss syndrome.61 Not every promising clinical use panned out.

    What actually matters¶

    For a healthy person who lifts, creatine is close to a free lunch: creatine monohydrate, 3–5 g every day, with no loading and no cycling,39 taken consistently and paired with resistance training for the muscle benefit,1 from a third-party-tested product (Informed-Sport / Creapure) because roughly 15% of loosely-regulated supplements have been found contaminated with undeclared substances.58 Expect modest, real strength and lean-mass gains over months (not a dramatic overnight change once the initial water settles), possible cognitive help if you are older, vegetarian or sleep-deprived, and none of the harms the myths promise. What it will not do is build muscle without training, boost testosterone, cure a disease, or wreck your kidneys or hairline.

    One mechanistic footnote worth knowing: your body makes creatine from the amino acids glycine, arginine and methionine, and this synthesis consumes a striking ~40% of the body's methyl groups (from methionine/SAM) — which is part of why vegetarians, who must make nearly all their own creatine, respond the most to supplementation.59
